Burying burnt wood after wildfires could help the climate | CBC Climate Change News

Date: 2026-04-02

Short summary: Burying burnt wood after wildfires could help the climate

Full summary: A University of Maryland climate scientist, Ning Zeng, has found that burying dead wood after wildfires can help lock away stored carbon. The discovery was made after Zeng and his team buried an Eastern red cedar log in a farm field outside of Montreal and found it to be mostly intact after 3,700 years. The log contained 95 per cent of its stored carbon, and Zeng believes that this method could be used to prevent gigatons of carbon from being released into the atmosphere. A company called Mast Reforestation has already led a commercial-scale wood burial project in Montana, burying 10 million pounds of trees that were burnt in a 2021 wildfire. However, some researchers have raised concerns about the potential impact on ecosystems. The method involves excavating a pit, piling in the trees, and covering them with a clay cap, gravel, and topsoil. Sensors are also set up to ensure no carbon is leaking from the bunker.
